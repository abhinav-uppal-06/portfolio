Market Strategy 6 min read Kempower · Italy · EV Infrastructure

Italy Has a Policy Problem Disguised as an Infrastructure Problem

When I started working on the Kempower engagement, I assumed Italy's slow EV adoption was a supply problem not enough chargers, not enough investment. I was wrong. The chargers are there. The investment is coming. The problem is something far more structural, and far more interesting.

Published
April 2026
Project
Kempower Oyj Italian Market Entry
UCD Smurfit · Oct–Nov 2025
Frameworks Used
VRIO Porter's Five Forces CAGE Market Sizing

When I started working on the Kempower engagement, I assumed Italy's slow EV adoption was a supply problem. Not enough chargers, not enough investment. I was wrong on both counts. The bigger surprise was something I did not expect to find at all: a country divided sharply in two, where the gap between north and south tells you almost everything about why EU climate policy so often fails at the last mile.

Kempower Oyj is a Finnish EV charging manufacturer, founded in 2018, with over 800 employees and market leadership in Norway. Italy was the next expansion target. Our task was to build the market entry strategy. And what struck me almost immediately when I started mapping the infrastructure data was this: northern Italy looks nothing like southern Italy when it comes to EV readiness. Milan, Turin, Bologna and the industrial north have relatively developed charging corridors, commercial adoption, and grid capacity. Go south of Rome and the picture changes entirely. Sparse infrastructure, older grids, lower average incomes, and far less commercial incentive for operators to invest.

Italy has clear EU 2030 carbon neutrality obligations. The funding is flowing. The government is formally committed to electrification. And yet this rich-poor divide within the country means that aggregate national targets mask a structural problem underneath. The south is being left behind, and no amount of Brussels policy language changes that without targeted, ground-level intervention.

The Gap Is Structural, Not Technical

What I found when I mapped Italy's charging infrastructure against its economic geography was a mismatch that policy alone cannot fix. The mandate exists. The money exists. But in the Mezzogiorno, the commercial incentives for infrastructure operators simply do not stack up the same way they do in Lombardy. Lower vehicle ownership rates, lower average daily kilometres driven, weaker grid infrastructure, and a different business culture all combine to make EV charging investment less attractive in the south even when subsidies are available.

The A22 motorway freight corridor in the north is one of Europe's busiest north-south trucking routes, and it had almost no MCS-ready charging infrastructure when we conducted our analysis. That is where the money is. That is where Kempower should focus first. Trying to solve the south before you have established commercial footholds in the north is a strategic error that several competitors have already made.

"The gap between EU policy and Italian market reality is not a technology problem. It is a coordination problem, and coordination problems are solved by partnerships, not mandates."

What the Entry Strategy Actually Required

Our recommendation was a partnership-led market entry rather than a direct infrastructure build. Italy's business culture is relationship-oriented in a way that makes unilateral entry expensive and slow. The smarter move was to enter through established operators who already had the grid connections, the client relationships, and the local regulatory knowledge.

We identified three partnerships: TSG Italia for e-bus charging in Bologna, Enel X for Dynamic Power Management depots in Reggio Emilia and Piacenza, and Free To X for Megawatt Charging System infrastructure on the A22 freight corridor near Verona. Each partner unlocked a different market segment without requiring Kempower to build local commercial infrastructure from scratch.

The VRIO analysis showed that Kempower's real competitive advantage was not the hardware. Competitors like ABB and Siemens have comparable products. The advantage was the Dynamic Power Management software and the Megawatt Charging System capability, both of which address Italy's specific grid constraint problem and are genuinely difficult to replicate at scale.

The Broader Lesson

I came away from this project with a sharper understanding of how regional inequality shapes market strategy in ways that national-level analysis completely misses. Italy is not one market. It is at least two, and probably more. Any entry strategy that treats it as homogeneous will underperform. The north-south divide is not going to close quickly, and the businesses that succeed in Italy over the next decade will be the ones that design for that complexity rather than pretending it does not exist.

This article draws on analysis conducted as part of a UCD Smurfit MSc Management Consultancy group engagement with Kempower Oyj, Oct to Nov 2025. All strategic recommendations are those of the student consulting team.

Organisational Behaviour 5 min read Ecomm Edge · Change Management · Burnout

When a "Scalability" Decision Destroys the Thing It Was Meant to Scale

In November 2025, Ecomm Edge made a structural change to its sales operation that looked sensible on paper. Remove the pre-qualifier role, consolidate the funnel, reduce headcount costs. Within weeks, closing rates had halved. Here's what actually happened and why it keeps happening across industries.

April 2026
Project
Ecomm Edge Motivation & Change Diagnostic
UCD Smurfit · Oct–Dec 2025
Frameworks Used
JD-R Model Change Management OB Diagnostics

In November 2025, Ecomm Edge made a structural change to its sales operation that looked sensible on paper. Remove the pre-qualifier role, consolidate the funnel, reduce headcount costs. Within weeks, closing rates had halved. What surprised me most was not that the performance dropped. It was that the drop was entirely predictable, and yet nobody at the top saw it coming.

Ecomm Edge is India's premier D2C e-commerce accelerator, serving over 10,000 entrepreneurs. Its sales team operated with a clear division of labour: pre-qualifiers warmed up leads and filtered out weak prospects before handing them to specialist closers. The closers closed. That was their whole job. Average closing rates were around 32%. Show-up rates sat at 75%. Revenue was growing.

Then leadership removed the pre-qualifier layer. The closers would now handle the full funnel themselves: screening incoming leads, qualifying prospects, and then closing them. Same salary. No new training. No adjusted targets. No transition support. Just a new job description and a quiet expectation that performance would continue as before.

By December, closing rates had dropped to 14%. Show-up rates fell to 55%. Revenue declined sharply from October onwards. Leadership's read was that the closers were not adapting. Our read, after talking to the sales team directly, was entirely different.

What Should Have Been Obvious

Here is the thing that stayed with me after this project: the outcome was not a surprise. The moment you triple someone's workload without giving them the tools, training, or compensation to handle it, you are not making them more productive. You are burning them out. That is not a subtle insight from organisational behaviour theory. It is common sense. And yet the management team at Ecomm Edge did not appear to have modelled it at all.

When I ran the employee wellbeing data through the Job Demands-Resources framework, the picture was clear. Workload scores hit 90%. Confidence had fallen to 50%. Autonomy was at 40%. Role clarity sat at 30%. These are not the numbers of a team that is adapting slowly. These are the numbers of a team that has been set up to fail and knows it.

"The closers were not failing. The system was failing them. That distinction matters enormously when you are deciding what to fix."

Why the Experienced People Struggled Most

One of the more telling findings was that the newest closer, Aditya, maintained his performance through the transition while the experienced closers saw sharp drops. Leadership interpreted this as evidence that the change was workable. I interpreted it differently. Aditya adapted easily because the new model was the only model he had ever known. He had no previous workflow to unlearn. The experienced closers had to simultaneously abandon a successful habit, acquire a new one, and meet unchanged performance targets. Under pressure. Without support. That is a recipe for disengagement, not adaptation.

The change also happened without any consultation with the sales team. It was announced and implemented. No pilot period. No feedback loop. No co-design. The people most affected by the decision had no input into it. That alone significantly increases the probability of resistance and disengagement, regardless of whether the underlying change is strategically sound.

What Good Change Management Would Have Looked Like

Our recommendations were practical. Deploy an AI or WhatsApp chatbot to handle lead pre-qualification automatically: this restores the filter without the headcount cost that prompted the original change. Bring visible leadership back into the sales floor because the data showed a direct link between mentor presence and performance. Build a centralised procedures document so people have a reference point when their role shifts.

The broader lesson I took from this is one that applies well beyond D2C sales. When organisations restructure for efficiency, they almost always underestimate the transition cost. The change might be the right one. But the way it is introduced determines whether it succeeds or fails. In this case, the decision received significant strategic thought. The implementation received almost none. That gap is where performance goes to die.

This article draws on analysis conducted as part of a UCD Smurfit Organisational Behaviour module group engagement, Oct to Dec 2025.

Corporate Donors Don't Fund Causes. They Fund Business Cases.

The problem wasn't the impact. It was the pitch.

The moment that pulled me towards consulting was working on the RCED project through 180 Degrees Consulting at Motilal Nehru College. An organisation that had spent 30 years building real impact 50,000 entrepreneurs trained, 35,000 skill development trainees, partnerships with 8 government ministries and yet struggling to raise corporate funding. The surprise was not that the funding was hard to find. The surprise was why: RCED had no way to measure what its volunteers were actually doing, and almost no visibility beyond its immediate geographic base.

RCED (Rural Community Enterprise Development) is an Indian non-profit focused on entrepreneurship training and skill development for underserved communities. Its track record on paper is genuinely strong. Thirty years of operation, government credibility, a large volunteer network. And yet when I started asking about how they tracked volunteer contribution, how they measured programme outcomes per district, and how they reported impact to external stakeholders, the answers were either vague or non-existent.

That is the core problem. Corporate donors do not fund good intentions. They fund evidence. And RCED, despite doing significant work, had very limited ability to show that work in terms a corporate CSR committee could evaluate.

The Measurement Problem

The volunteer issue surprised me most. RCED runs a large portion of its programmes through volunteer delivery, which is actually a strength in terms of cost efficiency and community trust. But there was no consistent framework for recording what each volunteer did, how many people they reached, what outcomes followed, or how their contribution mapped to the organisation's broader goals. This meant that a huge amount of genuine activity was essentially invisible in any formal reporting sense.

For a corporate donor, this is a red flag. If you cannot tell me how many people Volunteer X reached in District Y last quarter, I cannot put that in my CSR report. And if I cannot put it in my CSR report, I cannot justify the spend to my board. The gap between what RCED was doing on the ground and what it could demonstrate to funders was significant, and it was entirely fixable.

"RCED was doing the work. It just could not prove it in the language that unlocks funding. That gap is a consulting problem, not a mission problem."

The Reach Problem

The second surprise was geographic. RCED's impact was real but concentrated. Its programmes were well established in certain regions, but awareness among potential corporate donors, particularly those operating in different parts of India, was low. The organisation had not invested in visibility beyond its immediate network. This meant it was largely invisible to the corporate CSR teams it most needed to reach.

This is a common pattern with established nonprofits of RCED's generation. They built their credibility through fieldwork, not communications. That was the right priority at the time. But the funding landscape has shifted. Corporate CSR budgets are now managed more rigorously, competition for them is more intense, and the organisations that win are the ones with the clearest digital and narrative presence alongside their programme delivery.

What the Pitch Deck Had to Solve

Our 180DC team built a CSR fundraising pitch deck designed specifically for corporate audiences. The reframe was fundamental: instead of presenting RCED as a charity seeking support, the deck positioned it as a strategic CSR partner with a proven delivery model and government-validated credibility.

We built a simple impact metrics framework: cost per entrepreneur trained, programme completion rates, geographic coverage, government partnership count. We mapped RCED's volunteer activity against a proposed measurement structure so future reporting would be consistent. And we used the 8 government ministry partnerships as the credibility anchor, because government relationships signal legitimacy and compliance in a way that private endorsements cannot.

The organisations with the most genuine impact often have the weakest fundraising infrastructure. That is not a criticism of the people running them. They are doing the hard work. But the gap between impact and fundable narrative is one of the most solvable problems in the social sector, and it is the one I keep coming back to.

This article draws on consulting work conducted through 180 Degrees Consulting, Motilal Nehru College Chapter, 2023 to 2024.
